Break Boundaries, Bend Stereotypes:

Gender Equality for All

"Equality isn’t about making everyone the same — it’s about freeing everyone to be their true selves, beyond stereotypes and limits."

Gender equality is defined as every individual having the same rights, and opportunities, regardless of gender. It means that both men's and women's needs, interests, and objectives are fairly taken into account, acknowledging the difference among various social groupings. In order to guarantee that no one is disadvantaged or privileged because of their gender, this idea is not just for women; it also applies to males, transgender, and gender nonconforming individuals.

.

Addressing stereotypes, empowering women and both individual and policy actions are all important components of a numerous strategy for promoting gender equality in society. One successful strategy to advance gender equality is to eliminate and alter gender role stereotypes in society and families, support equal distribution of rights, and create conditions that allow both men and women to thrive (UNFPA, 2023).

Education is significant in encouraging gender-sensitive curricula and removing gender stereotypes from educational materials can help have awareness and change in attitudes from a young age (Doherty, 2022). Men must embrace respectful relationships and support efforts for gender equality in order to fulfil their essential role (Martin, 2023). Speaking out against gender-based discrimination can establish a more equal society, promoting inclusive policies, and assisting women-owned enterprises (United Way NCA, 2025).

A long-term change requires tackling systemic problems including child marriage, sexual harassment, and legal constraints that prevent women from participating fully in the economy (Martin, 2023). Helping empower women to reach their full capabilities requires supporting women-led organizations and making sure women have access to leadership opportunities (Global Goals, 2024).

.

In the 2024 Global Gender Gap Index (GGGI), the Philippines ranked 25th out of 146 countries, with a gender parity score of 77.9% (Dealca, 2025). This indicates that the country has closed nearly 78% of its gender gap. Much of this progress is due to equal access to education, health, and professional opportunities, alongside strong legal protections for women, supported by the Magna Carta of Women (RA 9710) and the recently passed SOGIE Equality Bill (Dealca, 2025; Philippine Commission on Women, 2024; Nicolas, 2024).

In contrast, the Philippines ranked 70th out of 139 countries in the 2024 SDG Gender Index by Equal Measures, with a score of 67.52 out of 100, one of the lowest in the region. Despite achievements in gender parity, the country faces persistent practical issues, such as violence against women and limited access to reproductive health services, which contribute to its lower SDG Gender Index ranking (Abbott & Abbott, 2024).

This contrast highlights that while the Philippines performs well in closing gender gaps, it still struggles with real-world outcomes that affect the daily lives of women and queer individuals. To bridge this gap, collective action is needed through policy enforcement, advocacies, and inclusive community efforts, to ensure that legal equality translates into lived equality for all.
